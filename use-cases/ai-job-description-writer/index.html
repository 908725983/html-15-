The Problem

What Is an AI Job Description Writer?

Job descriptions are the most-read and least-optimized content in most companies. They are written once, copied from a template last updated in 2019, edited minimally for each new role, and posted without a second thought. Yet the JD is the single biggest factor in who applies — and who doesn't. Research from LinkedIn Talent Solutions shows that 77% of candidates read the job description before deciding to apply, and Textio's analysis of 75 million JDs found that gendered language alone can reduce the applicant pool by up to 40%. The problem compounds at scale: a company hiring for 50 roles is spending 200 hours on JD writing and unknowingly embedding bias into every single one.

Existing solutions don't solve the root cause. HR teams rely on outdated JD libraries — Word documents on a shared drive, last edited by someone who left the company two years ago. Some use template platforms that standardize formatting but offer zero language analysis. Others paste basic requirements into ChatGPT and get back generic output that sounds like every other JD on the internet — with no bias screening, no SEO optimization, and no channel adaptation. None of these approaches address the three bottlenecks that determine whether a JD attracts or repels the right candidates: language inclusivity, search discoverability, and channel-specific optimization.

An AI job description writer is fundamentally different. It is not a template filler or a text generator — it is a hiring intelligence system. COCO's AI JD writer connects to your ATS and careers page, ingests your company values and culture documentation, and produces job descriptions that are bias-free (scored on a 0-100 inclusivity scale), SEO-optimized (researched against what candidates actually search on Google for Jobs, LinkedIn, Indeed, and Glassdoor), channel-adapted (platform-native versions that feel authentic to each channel's audience), compensation-benchmarked (real-time market data from Payscale, Glassdoor, and Levels.fyi integrated directly into the JD), competency-structured (organized around outcomes and what success looks like, not just years of experience), and compliance-checked (validated against EEOC guidelines, EU Pay Transparency Directive, and local labor laws). Hiring managers go from spending 4 hours per JD to 10 minutes — while the JDs they publish attract 64% more applicants with 41% more diverse candidate pools.

Limitations & Caveats

  • Results vary by industry, role type, company brand strength, and local labor market conditions. Organizations with strong existing employer brands may see smaller relative gains (because they already attract more applicants). Organizations in talent-scarce markets may see larger gains in applicant volume but smaller gains in time-to-fill due to structural supply constraints.
  • Demographic data on applicant gender and ethnicity is based on optional self-reporting in ATS systems. Not all candidates choose to self-identify, creating potential undercount bias. The +41% female candidate figure should be interpreted as a directional improvement, not a precise measurement. Reporting completeness varies by geography due to different legal frameworks for collecting demographic data.
  • The inclusive language engine detects known patterns of biased language based on academic research (Gaucher, Friesen & Kay, 2011; Mohr, 2014) and industry data (Textio, LinkedIn). It does not guarantee elimination of all bias — language bias is culturally and contextually specific, and new patterns emerge as workplace language evolves. We recommend regular review of inclusivity scores and flagged terms as part of ongoing DEI program management.
  • Compensation benchmarking data from Payscale, Glassdoor, and Levels.fyi reflects self-reported and employer-reported data, which may have sampling biases (e.g., tech industry overrepresentation on Levels.fyi). We recommend cross-referencing with internal comp data and third-party compensation surveys specific to your industry and region for executive and highly specialized roles.
  • SEO optimization for job boards depends on platform algorithm changes. Google for Jobs, LinkedIn, Indeed, and Glassdoor periodically update their search and ranking algorithms. COCO's SEO engine is updated quarterly to reflect algorithm changes, but month-to-month fluctuations in search visibility are normal and expected. We recommend monitoring JD performance metrics monthly.

How does COCO eliminate bias in job descriptions?
COCO's inclusive language engine scans every job description across five distinct bias dimensions, backed by over a decade of academic research on linguistic bias in hiring. The gendered language detector flags male-coded terms like "rockstar," "ninja," "dominant," and "competitive" — which research from Textio and the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology shows reduce female applicants by up to 40% — as well as female-coded terms that can inadvertently signal lower authority. The age bias detector catches phrases like "digital native," "young and energetic," and "recent graduate" that violate EEOC age discrimination guidelines. The ableist language detector identifies unnecessary physical requirements ("must be able to lift 50 lbs" for a desk job) and sensory-biased phrasing ("walk the floor," "must have clear speaking voice"). The cultural bias detector flags terms like "native English speaker," "clean-cut appearance," and "cultural fit" that signal homogeneity expectations. The credential inflation detector identifies when "required" qualifications are actually preferences that disproportionately screen out candidates from non-traditional backgrounds. Each flagged term comes with a specific, contextually appropriate rewrite suggestion and an explanation of why the original language was flagged — so hiring teams learn to write more inclusively over time, not just accept AI corrections. Every JD receives a 0-100 inclusivity score, and teams can set minimum score thresholds before a JD is approved for publishing. This is not a simple find-and-replace — it is a linguistic analysis engine trained on 75 million job descriptions and the academic research on how specific language patterns affect who applies.
